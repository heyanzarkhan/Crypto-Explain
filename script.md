# Crypto, Explained — Narration Script

Target length: ~8 minutes. Voice: calm, clear, conversational.
Use commas and periods for natural pauses. Ellipses (…) mark slightly longer pauses for visual reveals.

---

## 1. INTRO  (≈ 14 s)

> What is cryptocurrency. What is a blockchain. And how can people send money across the world without a bank.
>
> In the next few minutes, you will understand all of it. Let's start from zero.

---

## 2. WHAT IS CRYPTOCURRENCY  (≈ 70 s)

> Money, as you know it, comes from a bank or a government. You hold rupees, dollars, or euros … but the rules are made by someone else.
>
> Cryptocurrency is different. It is digital money that lives only on the internet. No bank prints it. No government controls it.
>
> Instead, it lives on a network of computers all over the world. The most famous one is Bitcoin. But there are thousands of others … Ethereum, Solana, and many more.
>
> With crypto, you can send money to anyone, anywhere … in minutes. Without needing permission from a bank.

---

## 3. WHAT IS USDT  (≈ 55 s)

> Most cryptocurrencies have one problem. Their price moves up and down very quickly. One day a coin is worth a lot. The next day, much less.
>
> So how do you hold digital money without that risk? That is where USDT comes in.
>
> USDT, also called Tether, is what we call a stablecoin. One USDT is designed to always equal one US dollar.
>
> It moves on crypto networks like any other coin … but its value stays steady. People use USDT to save, to trade, and to send dollars across borders.

---

## 4. WHAT IS BLOCKCHAIN  (≈ 90 s)

> Now, the big question. If there is no bank … who keeps track of who owns what?
>
> The answer is the blockchain.
>
> Imagine a giant notebook. Every time someone sends crypto, a new line is written in this notebook.
>
> But this notebook is not stored in one place. Copies of it exist on thousands of computers around the world.
>
> Each page of this notebook is called a block. When a page fills up, it is linked to the page before it … forming a chain. A block-chain.
>
> Because everyone has a copy, no single person can cheat or change the records. To fake a transaction, you would have to change every copy, on every computer, at the same time. Which is nearly impossible.
>
> That is what makes the blockchain trustworthy.

---

## 5. TRANSACTIONS, HISTORY, AND WALLETS  (≈ 115 s)

> To use crypto, you need a wallet. A crypto wallet is not like a leather wallet. It is a small piece of software, or a tiny device, that holds your keys.
>
> Every wallet has two things. A public address … like an email, that anyone can send money to. And a private key … like a password, that only you should know.
>
> When you send crypto, that action is called a transaction. The transaction gets recorded on the blockchain forever.
>
> You can view any wallet's full history on a website called a block explorer. Every send, every receive, with date and amount … all public. But linked only to wallet addresses, not real names.
>
> This is why people say crypto is transparent … and yet, private.
>
> **But that has a catch.** Your wallet address stays the same. Forever. And every transaction you ever made stays on the blockchain — anyone can look it up.
>
> So a transaction you do today, with someone you trust, might later be traced … to something illegal that person did. And suddenly your wallet is linked to it.
>
> Your past is public. And permanent. That is something you should always remember.

---

## 6. WHAT IS P2P  (≈ 85 s)

> P2P stands for peer to peer.
>
> In the normal world, if you want to send money, a bank sits in the middle. You ask the bank. The bank moves the money.
>
> In a peer to peer system, there is no middleman. You connect directly to the other person. Computer to computer.
>
> Crypto is peer to peer by design. When you send Bitcoin to a friend … it does not pass through any company. It goes directly, through the blockchain network.
>
> This makes crypto fast, global, and open to everyone … with no gatekeepers.
>
> **But — a peer can be anyone.** You may not know who is really on the other side. Platforms often do not verify their users.
>
> If the person you send crypto to is doing something illegal … your wallet now has a link to theirs. And that could put you in real trouble.
>
> Always know who you are dealing with.

---

## 7. OUTRO  (≈ 25 s)

> So. Crypto is digital money. The blockchain is the public notebook that tracks it. A wallet holds your keys. Transactions are recorded forever … and they are public. USDT is a stable version of crypto. And P2P means no middleman — but always verify who you deal with.
>
> That is the foundation. The rest of crypto builds from here.
